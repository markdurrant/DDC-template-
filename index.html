Folow the money

Examinging the influence of the media on the level of aid following humanitarian crises.

At the Development Data Challenge in London, a group of developers, journalists, social entrepreneurs and international development experts set themselves the challenge of tracking the influence of media coverage on the amount of funding following natural disasters. We selected six major natural disasters from the last ten years; the Indian Ocean Tsunami (2004), Hurricane Katrina (2005), the Haiti Earthquake (2010), the Pakistan Floods (2010) the Japan Earthquake and Tsunami (2011) and the East Africa Drought (2011).

  • Haiti Earthquake

    January 2010 Many The earthquake that hit Haiti at 16:53 local time on 12 January 2010 was relatively smaller than others measuring 7.0 in magnitude. However, heavily urbanised areas close to the epicentre as well as existing poverty, governance and health problems meant that the impact was larger in Haiti than similar sized earthquakes elsewhere.

  • Pakistan Floods

    July 2010 A gazillion After heavy monsoon rains, in July 2010 Pakistan was hit by its worst floods for over 80 years causing serious damage to the country’s power infrastructure and leaving victims inaccessible from relief efforts. The submerged land included some of Pakistan’s most fertile areas meaning that agricultural crops and local jobs saw huge losses.

  • Japanese Earthquake and Tsunami

    March 2011 Many At 14:46 local time on 11 March 2011 the world’s fifth strongest recorded earthquake shook Japan and was followed by over 100 aftershocks. The earthquake created a tsunami in the Fukushima region in the north-east of the country. Fukushima’s huge nuclear power plant was severely damaged by the earthquake resulting in a series of explosions which posed a serious health risk to the region’s residents.

  • East Africa Drought

    July 2011 A gazillion Severe drought in the Horn of Africa which began in July 2011 has resulted in food crises and ensuing health and migratory emergencies in several East African countries. Djibouti, Ethiopia, Kenya and Somalia have been deeply affected as well as Sudan, South Sudan and Uganda. UN defined famine (when there is a complete lack of food access and mass starvation, death and displacement) was declared in south Somalia in 2011.

  • Hurricane Katrina

    August 2005 A gazillion On 29 August 2005, at 07:10 Hurricane Katrina hit the Florida coast after gathering strength for days near the Gulf of Mexico. Storm winds continued to grow in size and travelled through Louisiana, Mississippi and Alabama leaving property damage, oil spills and flooding in their wake.

  • Indian Ocean Tsunami

    December 2004 A gazillion At 07:59 local time on 26 December 2004, an 8.9 magnitude earthquake occured under the Indian Ocean. The world’s largest for at least 40 years, the earthquake triggered an enormous Tsunami reaching heights of 10m. The tsunami spread across the entire Indian Ocean, hitting several south Asian countries as well as the coastlines of Somalia, Kenya and Tanzania. Indonesia, where the quake’s epicentre was located, was the country worst-hit by this disaster.
